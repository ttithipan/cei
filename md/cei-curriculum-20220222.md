### 1.10 Essential situations or developments to be considered in program planning
#### 1.10.1 Economic situation/development

Amid the COVID 19 pandemic situation, the transformation to the digital economy is still an
essential change for the long-term development of the country. The digital economy, the

economy based on digital computing technologies, has become more important over the
years. Many objectives in the draft of the Thirteen National Economic and Social
Development Plan demonstrate the necessities of the transformation to the digital economy at
different levels. As automation technology progresses, the demand for low-skilled workers
constantly declines. Businesses need high-skilled workers who are well equipped with broad
skills to keep up with the global trends such as data-driven decisions and machine
intelligence. The COVID 19 pandemic also accelerates the situation. Businesses need to
adopt online approaches when physical contacts are restricted. Technological disruption in
the global economy may cause some businesses to fade out if they are unable to adapt their
organizations for digital transformation.
#### 1.10.2 Social and cultural situation/development

Inequality is a multidimensional issue in Thai society. The draft of the Thirteen National
Economic and Social Development Plan also expresses the same concern over the solutions
to this chronic problem, yet unable to implement them properly due to the lack of democratic
stability and the low effectiveness of the government in economic development.
### 1.11 Impacts from 1.10.1 and 1.10.2 on the development of the program and relevance to the institutional missions
#### 1.11.1 Impacts on the development of the program

Core courses of this program cover the fundamentals of many areas in computer
engineering to develop computer personnel with broad skills. In order to respond to
the demand for automation, the program also includes artificial intelligence and
machine learning in its core courses.
The main objectives of this program do not address the inequality issue directly. The
program, however, plans to allocate a part of its budget for student scholarships.
#### 1.11.2 Impacts on the institutional missions

The education in this program aligns with the institutional missions for KMITL.

## 2 Program-Specific Information

### 2.1 Philosophy, significance, and objectives of the program

#### 2.1.1 Philosophy

Research and education in computer engineering are important parts of the foundation
for the progress of the nation.
#### 2.1.2 Significance

This program aims to develop graduate students in computer engineering, in accordance with
the national qualifications framework for higher education and national economic and social
development plan, to respond to the workforce demand from both public and private sectors.
#### 2.1.3 Objectives

##### 2.1.3.1 Program objectives

1. To develop computer personnel with T-shaped skills, which cover a broad set of
theoretical foundations in mathematics, computer science, and practical skills in
information technology of their interests.
2. To develop ethical and social-responsible computer personnel that recognize the impact
of computer engineering solutions to both locally and globally contexts.
3. To develop computer personnel who can communicate effectively and work
collaboratively with other professionals.
4. To stimulate research and innovative work from students and academic staff.
5. To promote entrepreneurship in young computer professionals and encourage them to
apply engineering solutions for business opportunities.
##### 2.1.3.2 Program learning outcomes (PLOs)

This program adopts the student’s expected learning outcomes recommended by ABET’s
Criteria for Accrediting Engineering Programs.
Upon graduation, the student is expected to possess the following abilities:
1. an ability to identify, formulate, and solve complex engineering problems by applying
principles of engineering, science, and mathematics
2. an ability to apply engineering design to produce solutions that meet specified needs with
consideration of public health, safety, and welfare, as well as global, cultural, social,
environmental, and economic factors
3. an ability to communicate effectively with a range of audiences
4. an ability to recognize ethical and professional responsibilities in engineering situations
and make informed judgments, which must consider the impact of engineering solutions
in global, economic, environmental, and societal contexts
5. an ability to function effectively on a team whose members together provide leadership,
create a collaborative and inclusive environment, establish goals, plan tasks, and meet
objectives
6. an ability to develop and conduct appropriate experimentation, analyze, and interpret
data, and use engineering judgment to draw conclusions

7. an ability to acquire and apply new knowledge as needed, using appropriate learning
strategies

### 2.3 Learning outcomes after the completion of each academic year

After the completion of each academic year, students will be able to:

**Year 1:**
- Identify, analyze, and solve simple problems in general physics
- Identify, analyze, and solve problems in basic calculus
- Identify, analyze, experiment, and solve basic electrical circuit problems
- Identify, analyze, experiment, and solve simple problems in digital logic design
- Design, develop, and test computer programs with both procedural programming and object-oriented programming concepts

**Year 2:**
- Identify, analyze, and solve simple problems in ordinary differential equations, linear algebra, discrete mathematics
- Apply probability theory and statistics for data analysis
- Recall and explain the fundamentals of computer organization and computer networks
- Employ database systems for information management as well as analyze and model the relationship of data entities
- Recognize and apply fundamental data structures and algorithms in computer programming to solve problems systematically
- Recognize and apply the best practices in software development systematically
- Recognize the importance of web technologies and develop web applications that conform to the industry standards

**Year 3:**
- Design and develop simple embedded systems using microcontroller
- Recognize the fundamentals of operating systems and apply multithreading in computer programming
- Recognize the fundamentals of information security and apply related technologies in practice
- Recognize the foundation of artificial intelligence and apply theory and related technologies to solve practical problems
- Work as a part of a team, both as a leader and a team member

**Year 4:**
- Identify real-world problems that can be solved with applications of computer engineering
- Apply theoretical knowledge in mathematics to formulate and solve problems in computer engineering
- Work in team to design, build, and test prototypes with real-world applications
- Plan and manage computer engineering projects with systematic approaches
- Communicate the ideas and body of work in computer engineering both verbally and written
- Recognize the importance of work ethics and social responsibility

Applicants are expected to satisfy all the admission requirements specified in the Regulation
of King Mongkut’s Institute of Technology Ladkrabang on Undergraduate Study B.E. 2564.
#### 3.2.3 New students’ expected problems

Students may have insufficient knowledge of mathematics or skills in mathematical
problem solving.
Students may have difficulties in understanding lecture or in communicating with
colleagues or staff.
Students may be unfamiliar with college-level study. Particularly, they must be
responsible for their learning.
The new students who have moved from home to live in an accommodation close to
the university will typically have more freedom in living their lives and, consequently,
some of those students may have problems in managing their time and the balance
between study and other activities.
#### 3.2.4 Strategies to mitigate problems stated in 3.2.3

Evaluate the applicants’ knowledge and skills of English language and mathematics.

Provide remedial English language courses for the new students who are weak in
English.
Organize an orientation session for the new students, including an introduction to
university study and advice on planning their study and future careers.
Organize a pre-sessional course to lay foundational skills and knowledge for studying
in the program and get them accustomed to university lecture.
Provide an academic advisor for each student who is responsible for monitoring the
student’s academic progress and provide consultations to the student.

### 3.4 Field experience courses (internships or co-operative education)

All students in this program are required to take an internship at least once after their 3rd year
of study. Co-operative education is also available for any student who wants to gain more
field experience.
#### 3.4.1 Standard learning outcomes

By the end of internship, students will be able to:
- Know the organizational structure and the overall goals and operations of the
company/organization and the functions of the department/division/working group in
which the students did their internships
- Have a clear understanding of the assigned tasks and a clear plan to perform those
tasks
- Complete the assigned tasks with the time and resources given
- Be involved in software development activities and apply the knowledge and skills
learned from the class on their work
- Communicate with all groups of people that the student contacted with as part of their
internship
- Work effectively under the supervision of a supervisor and as part of a team
- Acquire new technical knowledge and skills that are useful in completing the assigned
tasks
#### 3.4.2 Time frame

- Summer semester between the 3rd year and 4th year of study for students who choose
capstone design project
- The first semester of 4th year of study for students who choose co-operative education
or overseas training
#### 3.4.3 Schedule

One semester


### 3.5 Requirements on project and research work

The program offers capstone design project 1 and 2, which allow the students to study, apply
theoretical knowledge, design solutions, and implement prototype to problems in computer
engineering.
#### 3.5.1 Standard learning outcomes

By the end of capstone design project 1 and 2, students will be able to:
- Analyze and propose solutions to problems in computer engineering
- Design and develop prototypes that satisfy project requirements
- Utilize modern computer engineering tools
- Work in groups, as either leaders or team members
- Communicate effectively in both speech and writing
#### 3.5.2 Time frame

The first and second semester of 4th year of study
#### 3.5.3 Credits

- Capstone Design Project 1: 3 (0-9-5)
- Capstone Design Project 2: 3 (0-9-5)
#### 3.5.4 Preparation

1. Let students form group of 2-3 people. With consult from supervisors, each group
chooses a topic for a capstone project.
2. Supervisors advise their students on how to conduct research and assessments.
3. Supervisors schedule supervision hour.
4. Each group presents the project progress to the project assessment committee for
suggestion and assessment.
5. Students submit their completed theses for evaluation.
#### 3.5.5 Evaluation

1. From discussion among students and supervisors, each group sets the project’s goals
and evaluation methods that align with course objectives.
2. Each group presents the project result to the evaluation committee.
3. The project evaluation committee concludes the result for each group.

#### 4.2.1 Morality and ethics

##### 4.2.1.1 Learning outcomes

1. Perceive the importance of values, morals, ethics, self-sacrifice, and honesty
2. Have discipline, punctuality, and self and social responsibility
3. Have a team-working skill, be a good leader and a good team member, and be able to
resolve conflicts and prioritize the problems arising in the team
4. Respect other people’s rights, listen to others’ opinions, and respect the value and the
honor of being human
5. Respect the rules and the regulations of the institution and the society
6. Be able to evaluate the effects of computer usage on individuals, organizations, and
societies
7. Possess academic and professional ethics
##### 4.2.1.2 Teaching strategies

- Emphasize disciplines, punctuality, and submission of assignments on time
- Encourage (Provide opportunities for) the students to participate in activities which
benefit the society and build up good moral and ethical attitudes
- Encourage proper classroom etiquettes and dress codes
- Praise and/or reward good conduct
- Discourage dishonesty and misconduct, including plagiarism, by introducing punitive
measures and warning the students the consequences of their misconduct
- Set a good moral and ethical example for the students


- Invite a speaker to lecture about moral and ethics
- Provide opportunities for group discussions
- Encourage the students to participate in group discussions and make them learn to be
open mind and listen to other people’s opinions
- Discuss the impact of the subject or the course of study on people, organizations, and
societies
- Teach the students about the professional ethics and related regulations, and stress the
consequences of misconduct and negligence in profession on organizations, societies,
and themselves
- Provide opportunities for team working
- Teach the students about intellectual property and be aware and realize the
seriousness of plagiarism
##### 4.2.1.3 Assessment strategies

- Assign group homework or projects to the students and evaluate their team-working
skill
- Observe and evaluate class attendance and punctuality by regularly performing
attendance checks
- Observe and evaluate classroom etiquettes and dress codes
- Be vigilant of plagiarisms in the students’ submitted work and promptly offer
warnings and/or enforce punitive measures
- Observe and evaluate the students’ responsibility and punctuality in submitting
homework assignments
#### 4.2.2 Knowledge

##### 4.2.2.1 Learning outcomes

1. Have acquired knowledge and understandings of important principles and theories of
the subjects in the field of study
2. Be able to analyze problems, understand and explain computing requirements, as well
as be able to apply knowledge, skills, and the usage of suitable tools for solving
problems
3. Be able to analyze, design, deploy, improve, and/or evaluate computing systems and
their components in accordance with the requirements
4. Be able to keep themselves up to date with academic progress and computing
evolutions, as well as their applications
5. Know, understand, and be interested in acquiring new knowledge and developing
their skills in computing continuously
6. Have broad knowledge in the field of study, to anticipate the changes and understand
the effects of new related technology
7. Have experiences in designing and developing software of real use
8. Be able to integrate the knowledge in their field of study with the knowledge in other
fields


##### 4.2.2.2 Teaching strategies

- Plan the syllabus to cover the necessary topics in the course within the given
timeframe
- Deliver the lecture with the aid of presentation slides and employ additional learning
resources, e.g., computer programs, audio/video materials, models, and prototypes
- Suggest additional reading materials
- Include practical training or exercises
- Assign homework or projects to provide the opportunities for the students to train
their skills related to the course (such as programming skills), as well as their
problem-solving skills and self-study skills
- Invite guest speakers from the industry
- Adopt a problem-based learning strategy to provide an opportunity for the students to
develop their problem-solving skills, to integrate and apply a wide-range of
knowledge, to collaborate, and to be self-motivated
- Discuss or explain the applications of what the students are studying so that the
students to understand the knowledge in the real-world context and realize the impact
of the knowledge on the society
- Discuss or explain recent advancement and problems in the subject to encourage the
students to be continuously seeking new knowledge and be inquisitive
- Include interesting case studies
- Continuously adapt the pace and the strategy of teaching by regularly evaluating the
students and/or obtaining feedback from the students
- Assign the students to write essays on the applications of what they are studying in
the course
##### 4.2.2.3 Assessment strategies

Evaluate the students’ knowledge and skills from
- Mid-term examination
- Final examination
- Regular quizzes
- Assignments (homework, term projects, etc.)
- Oral examinations

### 5.2 Verification of the standards of student achievements

#### 5.2.1 Verification of the standards of student achievements during study

- The program management committee (or a nominated committee) checks the score

the committee notifies the course instructor to recheck and/or provide explanations,
possibly including showing of marked exam papers or homework.
- In each year, the program management committee (or a nominated committee)
conducts a verification of the standards of student achievements on selected courses
using one or more of the following methods:
- Studying the course plan and course report
- Studying marked homework, exam papers, and/or student projects
- Interviewing some students in the class
- Interviewing the course instructor
#### 5.2.2 Verification of the standards of student achievements after graduation
- Request for feedbacks from the employers of the recent graduates
- Interview the recent graduates and the graduates who have been working for 2 years
or more

## 7 Program Quality Assurance

### 7.1 Regulatory standards

The program adopts the CUPT Quality Assurance (CUPT QA) system initiated by the
Council of the University President of Thailand. CUPT QA consists of two components, each
specifying a set of indicators that the program must satisfy. The first component consists of
the indicators which ensure that the program is operated in accordance with the standards set
out by the Thai Qualifications Framework for Higher Education (TQF). The second
component consists of several indicators adopted from the AUN Quality Assurance (AUN
QA) system, initiated by ASEAN University Network. The program is subject to a regular
quality assurance evaluation. The program is evaluated against the first component of CUPT
QA every year and against the second component at least once every 5 years.
The following groups of individuals are involved in the quality assurance process:
1. Curriculum revision committee is responsible for revising the curriculum of the
program in accordance with the requirements of TQF and CUPT QA. The committee
must include at least 3 members who are experts in computer engineering or a related
field, one of which should come from the industry.
2. Program management committee consists of the five program faculty members. The
committee is responsible for managing the program and preparing the Self-
Assessment Report (SAR) every year for quality assurance evaluation.
3. Program stakeholders are the groups of individuals with potential interest in the
program. The following groups of individuals have been identified as the stakeholders
of the program:
a. Students: the students who were studying in the program
b. Lecturers: the academic staff members who delivered or assisted in delivering
courses in the program
c. Alumni: the students who graduated from the program
d. Employers: the current or prospective employers of the graduates of the
program.
4. Quality assurance evaluators are responsible for evaluating the program in the
(annual) quality assurance evaluation. The evaluators must be qualified to evaluate the
program according to CUPT QA.


### 7.2 Graduates

The goal of the program is to produce capable computer engineers who could work in the
industry at the international level and be able to pursue a postgraduate study on advanced
topics in a computing-related field. As specified in the Qualification Standards for
Undergraduate Programs in Computing (TQF 1 - Computing) announced by the Ministry of
Education of Thailand, the graduates are expected to have the following characteristics:
1. Having morals and ethics; being humble; being a good citizen; being responsible for
oneself, the profession, and the society
2. Having basic knowledge in software engineering, both theoretically and practically at
a sufficiently good level; Being able to properly apply the knowledge in the
profession and for further study
3. Having up-to-date knowledge; Being inquisitive; Being able to further own
knowledge continually to improve oneself, the jobs, and the society
4. Can think and can do; Being able to choose a solution systematically and
appropriately
5. Having teamworking skills and management skills
6. Being able acquire new knowledge; Being able to communicate with others
effectively
7. Being proficient in the use of English language for communication; Being able to
utilize information technology effectively
8. Being able to analyze the user’s requirements and able to design, develop, deploy, and
improve computer software to meet the user’s requirements and so that it works
effectively within its working environment
9. Being able to analyze the impact of an application of computers on people,
organizations, and society, including the impact in the legal and ethical aspects
10. Being able to act as consultants on the use computer systems in organizations
11. Being able to manage information systems within organizations
12. Being able to develop small-scale computer programs of real use
Additionally, the graduates are expected to have the special characteristics stated in Section
4.1. These expected characteristics have been translated in the learning outcomes in the five
domains of learning as listed in Section 4.2. These learning outcomes act as a basis in the
design of the curriculum.
Being one of the stakeholders, the graduates act as an integral component of CUPT QA. The
graduates are involved the quality assurance process in the following ways:
- Feedbacks from the graduates are used in the revision of the curriculum and are
reported as part of the annual program management report.
- The capabilities of the graduates affect their performance in their jobs, which is
reported by their employers in the (annual) employer survey.
- In the long run, the graduate capability affects the reputation of the program which, in
turn, affects the employability of the graduates, one of the indicators in CUPT QA.


### 7.3 Students

Students are a major element in CUPT quality assurance process. The program is required to
have a clear and effective student intake policy and a system for monitoring students’
academic performance. The student graduation and failure rates and the student’s satisfaction
level are constantly monitored and considered when reviewing the program.
#### 7.3.1 Student intakes

The methods and criteria for admitting students have been chosen with the following goals:
- The admitted students have sufficient knowledge and skills to succeed in the program.
- The admitted students show interest in software engineering, or computing and
technology in general, are motivated to succeed, and understand the possible career
paths once they graduate. Experiences in computer programming are beneficial, but
not required.
- Students from various educational background (e.g., Thai schools, International
Schools, homeschool/non-formal education, etc.) both within Thailand and abroad can
apply and be considered.
- The application and selection of students should not be unnecessarily complicated and
costly, while still being effective.
To open the admission to students with different backgrounds, students are admitted through
at least two different channels, as summarized in the table below.

| Channel | Target Students | Qualifications to Consider |
|---|---|---|
| Quota Admission | Students with excellent records and/or special abilities from international schools, English programs, Thai schools, and abroad | 1. English language test score; 2. Test score or academic records on mathematical skills |
| National Central Admission | Thai high school graduates | Scores from national qualification tests |

#### 7.3.2 Student monitoring

From entering the university, each student is assigned an academic advisor who monitors the
student’s academic performance and provides advice on academic matters as well as any
matter that can potentially affects their study. Each academic advisor sets aside office hours
to offer consultations.
#### 7.3.3 Graduation and failure rates

As part of the quality assurance evaluation, the graduation and failure rates are constantly
monitored and reported every year. All records of students’ termination of study (either
withdrawal or failure) are kept and studied by the program management committee.


#### 7.3.4 Student satisfaction

Student satisfaction is feedbacked to the program management committee through the
course/lecturer satisfaction survey held at the end of every semester and the program
satisfaction.
### 7.4 Academic staff

Quality of academic staff is a crucial factor for the program to succeed. The Ministry of
Education of Thailand has enacted criteria on the qualifications of academic staff members in
universities and the requirements on the academic staff involved in a program. CUPT QA
stipulates further quality indicators regarding the academic staff in a program.
#### 7.4.1 Qualifications and quantity of academic staff

- There are at least 5 program faculty members in the program. Each program faculty
member must have an academic background related to the program, have a master’s
degree or higher or be in the position of Assistant Professor or higher, and have at
least one acceptable academic publication in the past 5 years.
- A lecturer for a course in the program can either be a permanent academic staff
member in the university or be a visiting lecturer from outside the university. Every
lecturer must have a master’s degree or higher or be in the position of Assistant
Professor or higher in the field that related to the course. A visiting lecturer with only
a bachelor’s degree may be allowed to teach in the program only if he/she has at least
6 years of work or teaching experience in the relevant topics.
- All lecturers are required to have proficient English language skills.
- The ratio of the number of students per one (permanent) academic staff member is
expected to be at 10 or lower.
#### 7.4.2 Expected workload and responsibilities of academic staff

- As a guideline, each academic staff member in the program is expected to teach 3 –
12 hours per week. In addition, each academic staff member is expected to be
involved in advising students projects, typically 1 – 5 project groups in a semester.
- All academic staff members are expected to be active in research and/or academic
services to external organizations in the area that are related to their fields of expertise
or the courses that they teach. This is to ensure that they have up-to-date knowledge in
the relevant fields.

## 8 Program Evaluation And Improvement

### 8.1 Evaluation of the effectiveness of teaching

#### 8.1.1 Evaluation of teaching strategies

- The lecturers and the program management committee study the student evaluation of
courses and lecturers to obtain feedbacks on the effectiveness of the teaching
strategies.
- The lecturers and the program management committee study the students’ scores to
determine whether the teaching strategies used were effective or not.
- The program management committee regularly organizes a knowledge-sharing
session for the lecturers to meet and share the experiences and opinions on different
teaching strategies used.
- The program management committee interviews the lecturer of a course which has
some other course as a pre-requisite to ask whether the students have sufficient
background knowledge and skills from the pre-requisite courses. If not, there could be
problems with the teaching strategies used in the pre-requisite courses.
#### 8.1.2 Evaluation of the lecturers’ skills in executing teaching strategies

- The lecturers and the program management committee study the student evaluation of
courses and lecturers to obtain feedbacks on the lecturers’ skills in executing teaching
strategies.
- The lecturers and the program management committee study the students’ scores to
determine whether the lecturers have sufficient skills in executing teaching strategies
- The program management committee interviews the lecturer of a course which has
some other course as a pre-requisite to ask whether the students have sufficient
background knowledge and skills from the pre-requisite courses. If not, there could be
problems with the teaching skills of the lecturers of the pre-requisite courses.
### 8.2 Overall evaluation of the program

The program is subject to the CUPT quality assurance process, which include the criteria in
all aspects of the program execution. The program is evaluated against the first part of CUPT
QA (containing the criteria required by the Office of the Higher Education Commission)
every year, and against all criteria in both parts of CUPT QA at least once every 5 years. The
evaluation committee includes qualified evaluators who are experts in computer engineering
or related fields.
### 8.3 Evaluation of program execution with respect to the program specification
The program management committee and the quality assurance evaluators evaluate the
program execution in each academic year with respect to the KPIs specified in Section
7.7 of this document.


### 8.4 Review of evaluation results and planning for improvements

The program management committee summarizes the operation of the program in each
academic year in the annual program report and the QA self-assessment report. These reports
contain a summary of the problems occurred, feedbacks from various groups of stakeholders,
as well as statistics on the students and the graduates. The committee uses this information,
together with comment and suggestions from the quality assurance evaluators, to revise the
program and/or plan for improvements.
