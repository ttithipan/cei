## Knowledge Before Job Application or Internship

### 1. Job Applicant — Know Yourself

- Weak points and strong points of yourself
- Skills and experience gained in university
- Special interests and main target of your career
- Personality and attitude on working

### 2. Job Title — Understand the Position

- Details of job position
- Main job description
- Qualifications of the applicants
- Useful skills: Hard Skills and Soft Skills
- Salary and welfare benefits

### 3. Company and Their Industry

- Company information (Vision, Mission, Corporate Culture)
- Type of business (products, customers) and business trends
- Competitors and market opportunity

### 4. Skills for Job Application

- Resume and Cover Letter
- Tips for job interview and answering HR questions (behavioral questions)
- Apply via job finding platforms: LinkedIn, other websites
- Polite manners and dressing for job interview

### 5. Technology and Tools for Job Finding

- Apply and access job finding websites: LinkedIn, Job Thai, Indeed
- Basic computer programs: Microsoft Office, Google Workspace
- Create personal profile to convince employers

### 6. Manners and Corporate Culture

- Polite and appropriate dressing to work places
- Internal communication of organization: formal e-mail sending
- Create teamwork and connection with colleagues

## Hard Skills vs Soft Skills

### What is a Hard Skill?

- **Technical skills** that are specific to a job and can be measured.
- Represent things you've learned to do or use — tools, technologies, or proficiencies.
- Demonstrate your ability to perform specific tasks.
- Example: coding, copywriting, working with machinery.
- Always listed in a resume or areas where you have certifications.

### What is a Soft Skill?

- **Personal qualities and traits** that are transferable to any type of job and impact how you work.
- Interpersonal traits you have developed over time.
- Show how you approach your work and interact with others.
- Help you accomplish your goals and thrive in diverse work environments.
- Example: communication, critical thinking, creativity, problem solving.
- Always refer to **"people skills"**.

## Regulations Fresh Graduates Should Be Aware Of

### 1. Labor Laws and Employee Rights

- Working hours generally do not exceed 8 hours per day (with designated breaks)
- Minimum wage laws and holidays, including at least 6–13 days off per year
- Rights to sick leave, personal leave, maternity leave, and public holidays

### 2. Dressing and Organizational Culture

- Maintain a professional and appropriate dressing based on the company's policies
- Show politeness and respect towards colleagues and supervisors

### 3. Safety and Hygiene Policies

- Use personal protective equipment when working in hazardous areas
- Follow emergency procedures and safety standards

### 4. Work Responsibilities and Job Expectations

- Follow instructions and adhere to company guidelines
- Complete tasks on time and maintain quality standards

### 5. Compensation and Incentive Policies

- Receive salary and bonuses according to company policies
- Employment stability and social security rights

### 6. Discipline and Conduct Policies

- Avoid tardiness (late arriving) and rule violations
- Keep confidentiality of company information and client data

## 6 Levels of English — CEFR Classification

CEFR = Common European Framework of Reference for Languages.

| Level | Label | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **A1** | Beginner | Basic phrases, simple introductions |
| **A2** | Elementary | Simple conversations on familiar topics |
| **B1** | Intermediate | Handle everyday situations, describe experiences |
| **B2** | Upper Intermediate | Discuss complex topics, understand main ideas of technical texts |
| **C1** | Advanced | Fluent, spontaneous communication; academic/professional use |
| **C2** | Proficient | Near-native mastery; understand virtually everything |

## Generation Gap in the Workplace

### Multiple Generations in 2026

| Generation | Birth Years | Age Range | Nickname / Traits |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Baby Boomer** | 1946–1964 | 61–79 | "The retired hipsters" — living life to the fullest, staying fit, digitally connected. Over 85% check smartphones daily. |
| **Gen X** | 1965–1980 | 45–60 | "Emotionally rich and ready to pay if it's worth" — highly adaptable, tech-savvy, emotionally stable. Leading consumers for luxury products. |
| **Gen Y (Millennials)** | 1980–1995 | 30–45 | "The Tired Generation" — tri-life crisis: work stress, financial stability, starting families. Seeking daily motivation and life balance. |
| **Gen Z** | 1996–2011 | 14–29 | "The Trendsetters of the Digital World" — drawn to freedom, online creators, TikTokers, influencers. |
| **Gen Alpha** | 2010–2024 | 1–15 | "Shaping the Future" — fully immersed in digital. Aspiring content creators. New roles will emerge for them. |
| **Gen Beta** | 2025–2039 | 0+ | Growing up with AI and technologies. Highly adaptive. AI integral to education, work, and lifestyle. Will shape the future for decades. |

### Key Takeaways

- Each generation has distinct values, communication styles, and work expectations
- In today's multi-generational workplace, adaptability and understanding across generations is a critical soft skill
- Gen Beta will be the first generation to grow up fully in the AI era — expect rapid changes in job types and work culture
